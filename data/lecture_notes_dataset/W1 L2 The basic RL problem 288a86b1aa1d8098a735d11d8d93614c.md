## Learning a Policy

Our agent should learn a **policy**, a function telling it which action to select in each state.

> A **policy $\pi _t(s,a)=\pi (A_t=a|S_t=s)$** is a probability distribution that tells us the probability of our agent selecting action $a$ in state $s$ at time-step $t$.
> 
> - For each state, the following is true: $\sum_{a} \pi_t(a|s) = 1$, sum of the probabilities of the actions you can take at a particular state will be 1.

<aside>
💡

**Deterministic policies,** where the probability of choosing one action is 1 while the probability of choosing any others in 0.

**Stochastic policies,** where our agent might choose between several different actions with different probabilities. 

</aside>

> **Rewards, $R_t$,** reward at time-step t.
> 
> - rewards can be negative or positive
> - starting at state $S_t$, $R_{t+1}$ is **the immediate reward**.
- We want our agent to learn a policy that maximises the **total discounted return ($\bold{G_t}$) -** the discounted sum of all future rewards.
    
    $$
    G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\gamma ^kR_{t+k+1} \\ G_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma ^k R_{t+k+2} \\ G_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma G_{t+1}
    $$
    
- Can represent the total discounted return as a recursive sum
- **Discounted factor $\gamma$,** the further in the future that something happens the less important we deem it to be. $\gamma \in [0,1]$
    - Prevents the sum from diverging
    - If $\gamma =1,$ $G_t$ is simply the sum of rewards earned after time-step $t$.

## What does an RL algorithm do?

- It should tell us how to **use experience** generated by our agent to **modify its policy** in order to **maximise the discounted return**.
    - an example of an RL algorithm: **Q-learning**

<aside>
💡

*New Definitions:*

**Value:** what is good for our agent in the long-term. Is the **expected value of the total discounted return.** 

**Model**: Is the representation of the environment and understand what the actions will do.

- often do not have access to this but can assist the process.
</aside>

> **Q-value** is the expected value of the total discounted return when the agent starts at state s and takes action a, and follows the policy $\pi$ thereafter.
> 
> - Actions with higher Q-values will lead to higher discounted returns
> - If we had access to all Q-values we could simply choose the action which has the highest Q-values in every state and this will give the **optimal policy**
> - Default with no information is to set the Q-value to 0.

**Explore Randomly vs Act Greedily**

- Examples of potential policies where you need to consider the idea of exploration vs exploitation.

## The Gold Gridworld Problem: Q-learning

![Screenshot 2025-10-10 at 11.28.26.png](W1%20L2%20The%20basic%20RL%20problem/Screenshot_2025-10-10_at_11.28.26.png)

At the start the agent knows only:

- What state it is in
- What actions are available
- Must learn everything through trial and error iteration

---

- Label our starting position as state 1
- We know nothing about the Q-value in each of these directions, so we set it to the default of 0.
- Using the policy of **act greedily,** we choose the state-action pair which has the maximum Q-value
    - all equal so we randomly pick. in this case 1, North. Reward is -1.

![Screenshot 2025-10-10 at 14.01.53.png](W1%20L2%20The%20basic%20RL%20problem/Screenshot_2025-10-10_at_14.01.53.png)

![Screenshot 2025-10-10 at 14.03.04.png](W1%20L2%20The%20basic%20RL%20problem/Screenshot_2025-10-10_at_14.03.04.png)

This transition tells us about the Q-value of the state action pair 1, North. We update the value through the following:

$$
\begin{align*}
Q(1, N) &\leftarrow (1 - \alpha) Q(1, N) \\
        &\quad + \alpha \big( -1 + \max\{ Q(2, N), Q(2, S), Q(2, W), Q(2, E) \} \big)
\end{align*}
$$

- specific for this case, we are updating the Q-value of (1,N).
- obtain $Q(1,N) \leftarrow -0.1$

> **learning rate $\alpha$:** controls how much new information overides old information in the Q-value update.
> 
- choosing a learning rate of $\alpha=0.1$
    - The first term means we retain 90% of our old estimate of the state-action pair going North from state 1.
    - The second term introduces 10% new information combining the immediate reward of moving North: -1, and the best expected future value from state 2.
- This process continues iteratively
